August 2024 — Thank you for your consideration. My selected works tell all the details of my recent work, but that’s a lot to get through. Here’s a quick overview tailored specifically to the Staff Design Technologist role.

  • Develop working prototypes to explore new features, content strategies, and interaction models, ensuring the right questions are addressed and distinct user needs are met.
  • Facilitate decision-making and discussions by creating tangible artifacts that bring UX solutions to life, focusing on interaction and motion.

I love prototypes. I use Xcode daily and am currently learning Android Studio (thank you, David Morant). Despite the learning curve, I prefer to use the same tools as my engineering colleagues to easily work together and get as “real” as possible. I believe this will be especially helpful to SubX because I’ve learned that Newsreader’s legacy codebases sometimes lead to tricky challenges, which I’d uncover quickly.

For example, in Swift Tales 3, I show how I built a fully custom context menu prototype in SwiftUI to demonstrate GeometryReader techniques. While it was a cool prototype, it led me to ask the right question: Can we even build this on time? The answer was a resounding no. I used this code to gain agreement between iOS engineers, Jason Fujikuni, and Tiffany Pai to stick with Apple’s native Menu for now.

Alternatively, I recently helped Sandy Zhang build a low-fidelity prototype in about 2 hours to demonstrate her seemingly complex pattern of using navigation inside of a bottom sheet. We learned that programming the interaction and motion was surprisingly easy. She’s continuing her designs in Xcode.

  • Partner with designers and researchers to uncover user challenges and use these insights to inform your UX decisions.

I regularly use my code and design experience to uncover accessibility and usability challenges through prototyping. For example, I recently prototyped and 4 new user onboarding screens for Mitch Atkin to demonstrate VoiceOver techniques which required adding builds to my own phone.

Although I don’t get to work with researchers at the Times often, I have extensive research experience. At Robinhood, I conducted in-person field research for 2 weeks in Brazil to gain insights to inform a brand-new crypto app design. At Airbnb, I conducted usability testing with Support Specialists several times to inform my design decisions at several fidelities.

  • Ensure a seamless connection between design and engineering by maintaining design/UX visions through code, applying technical architecture and data to create realistic user and journalistic scenarios in prototypes.

In my 10 years of coding and design experience, I’ve found that acknowledging constraints early yields exceptionally well-crafted products. I’d love to apply this thinking in SubX.

Swift Tales 1 tells how I translated typographic line height into iOS, a detail I care much about as a designer. I only uncovered the niche quirks, and even a 10-year-old bug, by deeply understanding code and design together.

On the TPL team, I ensure our components work across diverse technical architectures. For example, Dialog handles presentation in UIKit so that the fade-in animation works correctly in the Newsreader and Audio apps.

  • Contribute to the continuous improvement of design standards, guidelines, and reusable components.

As an experienced designer of products and design systems, I know the joy of using systems that speed up work without sacrificing design quality. I’d love to help find that balance for SubX.

On the TPL team, I maintain a finite set of typography and color tokens across several platforms (read more about that in Swift Tales 1). However, I also work with teams to add the right amount of flexibility. For example, Dialog uses Text views that come with default styles but can easily be overridden with different typography tokes (see Swift Tales 3).
